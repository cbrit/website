I'm Collin. I'm a guy in his twenties who recently (less than a year ago) and accidentally started to pursue a career in DevOps engineering. Two years ago I didn't know what DevOps was. When I started at my first job after college, I was given The Pheonix Project as required reading, a novelized presentation of how to apply the Three Ways. From it I learned to identify and improve inefficiencies in IT organizations that I had never dealt with because, you know, I had just graduated. Nonetheless, it allowed me to enter the field of IT with some idea of what things should be like, which is invaluable if you want to make positive change in any field.

I shouldn't be deploying one-hundred VMs manually. I shouldn't be installing and patching this operating sytem on these baremetal servers manually. I shouldn't be configuring the networking, firewall, storage, permissions, etc. on said servers... manually. These kinds of thoughts were frequently crossing my mind a few months into my role in Operations. But everybody can identify problems; I decided I wanted to try and create solutions. So, I started learning about automation!

To be clear, automation is not DevOps, but it is certainly an important tool for the application of it. I had a foundational knowledge of scripting from college, so I was able to pick up PowerShell pretty quickly, which at the time of writing this continues to be my primary scripting language (sometimes a pain since I use a Mac,) though I am working on shell scripting in bash as well. Later I began learning Chef and all of its associated tools (not Chef Automate), which introduced me to concepts like idempotency, declarative configuration, infrastructure as code, and enough familiarity with Ruby to be dangerous.
